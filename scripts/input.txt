Habit one.
Create more than you consume.
This is the foundation.
And I want to explain why before I explain what.
Because this is not about productivity.
This is not about hustle culture.
It is about what passive consumption does to your psychology over time.
Here is the data first.
Thomas Corley spent years studying wealthy individuals for his book Rich Habits.
He found that 67% of wealthy people watch television for one hour or less per day.
Meanwhile 77% of financially struggling people watch more than one hour daily.
Now.
Before you feel personally attacked by a statistic…
This is not about television being the enemy of success.
It is about what excessive consumption trains your brain to do.
When you spend most of your time consuming…
You train your brain to be passive.
To receive.
To react.
To wait.
And that passive psychology does not stay in your Netflix habits.
It shows up in how you approach money.
Your career.
Your opportunities.
You wait for the raise instead of building the skill that justifies it.
You wait for the right moment instead of creating one.
You consume content about success while producing none of the conditions for it.

Until you realize you might be doing it right now.
So the habit.
Fifteen minutes a day creating something.
A channel. A skill. A project. An idea on paper.
And every week…
Audit your ratio honestly.
Creation time versus consumption time.
The number on the left needs to be winning.

Habit three.
Build an "I can do this" file.
This one sounds the simplest.
And it does the most invisible work of anything on this list.
Here is the psychology.
When a new challenge appears in your life…
Fear shows up.
Your brain immediately searches for evidence that you cannot handle it.
And without training…
It finds that evidence very quickly.
Because your brain's default setting is threat detection.
Not opportunity recognition.
The "I can do this" file changes the input.
Every time you achieve something meaningful…
Write it down.
Every milestone you reach…
Write it down.
Every moment you felt fear before doing something and did it anyway…
Write that down too.
And document what happened after.
Not just the achievement.
The feeling after the fear was wrong.
Because what you are building is a personal evidence archive.
A database of proof that you have faced hard things before.
And survived.
And usually thrived.
On the days when doubt is loudest…
And every doubt has a day when it is loudest…
You open the file.
And you let actual evidence speak louder than a feeling.
Your brain cannot argue with its own history.
